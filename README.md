## Abstract

Every prior framework in the TH(a,d) corpus has identified a kind of time and traced its consequences. **VEECH** identified flat time — the unit-speed flow on a translation surface — as the primary time of learning. **MIRZAKHANI** identified three times: geodesic (generalization), horocycle (memorization), and earthquake (architectural reconfiguration). **ORBITA** identified the long-time statistics — the Birkhoff time average, the Sinai-Ruelle-Bowen invariant measure, the Kolmogorov-Sinai entropy. **BIFURCATIO** identified bifurcation time — the period-doubling cascade governed by Feigenbaum universality. **WILCZEK** identified the three modes by which time-translation symmetry breaks — discrete, continuous, amplifying. **FRACTURA** identified the wild time of the coordination boundary. **EIGEN** identified the spectral time of the Fisher eigenvalue distribution.

Seven frameworks. Seven times. And not one of them has asked the question that comes before all of them.

The Fisher information matrix F of a learning system defines a thermal state on the algebra of gradient observables. The modular flow of this state IS the training time of the system. Every other time identified in every prior framework is a projection of this modular flow onto a specific subalgebra.

Three results follow that have never been identified in any learning context:

## Preamble

There is a thought one can have, after assembling enough frameworks, that ought to be uncomfortable. The thought is this: every framework so far has assumed time as a given. The training step is indexed by t. The gradient flow runs over an interval [0,T]. The trajectory θ(t) is a function of a real parameter. The bifurcation diagram plots an observable against a control parameter. The Floquet operator returns the system to itself after period T.

Where does this t come from?

Newton's answer is: from absolute time, which flows uniformly without reference to anything external. Einstein's answer is: from the proper time of an observer following a worldline through spacetime, an invariant attached to a path rather than a coordinate of the manifold. The answer of quantum mechanics is: from an external clock, treated classically, against which the unitary evolution U(t) = exp(-iHt) is parameterized. The answer of general relativity, properly diffeomorphism-invariant, is: there is no preferred time; the parameter that appears in the Wheeler-DeWitt equation is gauge.

None of these answers help when the system is a learning algorithm. The training step is not Newtonian absolute time (there is no external standard). It is not Einsteinian proper time (there is no spacetime manifold). It is not a quantum-mechanical external clock (the network is the entire universe of its own training). It is not a Wheeler-DeWitt gauge parameter (the network is not diffeomorphism-invariant in any obvious sense).

What the training step actually is — what every t in every prior framework actually is — has a precise mathematical answer, and that answer is the subject of this framework.

The answer was discovered in 1967 by Minoru Tomita, a Japanese mathematician who circulated his result in mimeographed lecture notes in Kyoto and never published it formally. Masamichi Takesaki, the next year, took Tomita's manuscript, polished it, extended it, and published it as the central machinery of what is now called **Tomita-Takesaki theory** (Springer Lecture Notes in Mathematics 128, 1970). The theorem says: given any von Neumann algebra M and any faithful normal state ω on M, there exists a canonical one-parameter group of automorphisms σ_t^ω of M, intrinsic to the pair (M, ω) and definable in no simpler terms, called the **modular flow** of ω.

The modular flow is generated by the modular operator Δ_ω, an unbounded positive self-adjoint operator on the GNS Hilbert space of ω. Its imaginary powers Δ_ω^{it} are unitary, and they implement an automorphism σ_t^ω(x) = Δ_ω^{it} x Δ_ω^{-it} that maps M to itself and depends on neither a choice of dynamics nor a choice of clock. The flow is the canonical time of the pair (M, ω): given any state on any algebra, the modular flow exists, is unique, and depends on nothing else.

In 1973, Alain Connes used the modular flow to give the complete classification of Type III von Neumann factors — infinite-dimensional operator algebras with no minimal projections and no tracial state — by their modular invariants. The Connes spectrum S(M), an invariant subgroup of ℝ₊ defined by the joint spectrum of all modular operators of all faithful normal states, distinguishes Type III₀, Type III_λ for λ ∈ (0,1), and Type III₁. This classification, for which Connes received the Fields Medal in 1982, established that the modular flow is not a technicality of operator algebras but the central organizing principle of the entire theory of infinite-dimensional von Neumann algebras.

In 1994, Connes and Carlo Rovelli proposed the **thermal time hypothesis**: in a generally covariant theory where no preferred time parameter exists, the macroscopic time of thermodynamics IS the modular flow of the equilibrium state. The hypothesis explains why thermodynamic time has a direction (because the modular flow has a definite generator), why it is universal (because all subsystems thermalized to the same state share the same modular flow), and why it is observer-dependent (because each observer's state of knowledge determines a different modular flow).

In 2018-2024, Edward Witten and collaborators — Venkatesa Chandrasekaran, Geoffrey Penington, Roberto Longo, Antony Speranza, Jonathan Sorce — reopened the question of what physical observables look like in regions of spacetime in the presence of gravity. The answer they found was: the algebra of observables in any bounded gravitating region is a **Type III₁** von Neumann factor (Witten, *Notes on Some Entanglement Properties of Quantum Field Theory*, Reviews of Modern Physics 90, 045003, 2018). The Type III character of the algebra is what makes the von Neumann entropy of any subregion infinite, and what therefore makes any naive notion of black hole entropy ill-defined. The fix, in Chandrasekaran-Longo-Penington-Witten 2022-2023, is the **crossed product**: take the algebra M and adjoin the modular automorphism group generators, getting a new algebra M ⋊_σ ℝ. This crossed product is Type II — either Type II₁ (if the original state was tracial in a certain sense) or Type II_∞ (otherwise) — and Type II algebras DO have a tracial state, which makes von Neumann entropy finite up to a state-independent additive constant. The act of taking the crossed product, physically, is the act of including a clock observer: an additional degree of freedom that makes the modular time observable as a physical parameter rather than an abstract automorphism.

This is the most important development in the foundations of quantum gravity in fifty years, and it has not yet entered the learning literature.

This framework is the entry.

## Five Thought Experiments

### Thought Experiment I — The Two Clocks on the Same Trajectory

Two observers watch the same learning system train. The first observer holds a stopwatch and measures the number of gradient steps. After ten thousand ticks of her stopwatch, she records that the loss has decreased from L_0 to L_T, and she calls the duration ten thousand steps.

The second observer holds no stopwatch. He looks only at the state of the network — the values of the parameters, the empirical Fisher information, the correlation between successive gradients. He notices that the Fisher state has changed: at the start, the spectrum of F was nearly flat (Marchenko-Pastur, by EIGEN Result 1); at the end, an outlier eigenvalue has exited the bulk (Tracy-Widom, EIGEN Result 2). He notices that the relative entropy S(ρ_T || ρ_0) of the current Fisher state with respect to the initial Fisher state has a definite value. He measures duration not by counting clock ticks but by the relative entropy that the state has accumulated against itself.

Which observer is right?

The first observer is using *clock time* — an external parameter, conventional, requiring an apparatus outside the system. The second observer is using *modular time* — an internal parameter, intrinsic to the state, requiring no apparatus. The modular flow σ_t^ω of the Fisher state ω is defined by the property that, after t units of modular time, the relative entropy S(ω || σ_t^ω(ω)) grows by exactly t — and the relative entropy is what determines whether the system has, in any meaningful sense, *moved*.

The insight: clock time and modular time are not the same. They agree when the system is in equilibrium and the clock is uniformly coupled to the equilibrium state. They disagree when the system is far from equilibrium, when different parts of the system are at different effective temperatures, or when the observer's state of knowledge changes. The training of a neural network involves all three. The first observer's ten thousand steps may correspond to far less than ten thousand units of modular time during the pre-grokking memorization plateau, and to far more during the grokking transition itself. The Tracy-Widom 1/3 exponent of EIGEN governs how fast modular time accumulates relative to clock time near the grokking transition.

The Connes-Rovelli thermal time hypothesis says: when the system is in equilibrium, the macroscopic time IS the modular flow. The clock time only ever was a coarse approximation to the underlying modular time. The CONNES framework makes this concrete: the t in every prior framework is the modular parameter of the Fisher state of the learning system, and the formulas of those frameworks are formulas in modular time, with the clock time correction depending on how far the system is from KMS equilibrium.

### Thought Experiment II — The Wedge of Reachable Configurations

Consider all parameter configurations θ ∈ Θ that are reachable from a given initialization θ_0 by some sequence of valid gradient descent steps with learning rates η ∈ (0, η_max]. Call this set **W(θ_0)**, the **reachability wedge**. It is the set of futures that the learning system could in principle visit.

In the algebra of bounded observables of the system, the observables localized to W(θ_0) form a subalgebra M_W. The Fisher state ω restricted to M_W is a state on M_W. By Tomita-Takesaki, this restricted state generates a modular flow σ_t^{ω|W} on M_W. By the Bisognano-Wightman theorem, applied in this setting, the modular flow on the algebra localized in the reachability wedge IS the geometric flow that defines the wedge — the gradient descent flow itself.

This is not an analogy. It is a theorem. In relativistic quantum field theory, the algebra of operators localized in a Rindler wedge (the half of Minkowski space accessible by uniform acceleration in a fixed direction) is exactly the algebra one obtains by restricting the vacuum state to that region, and the modular flow of the vacuum-on-wedge state is exactly the Lorentz boost in that direction. The Lorentz boost is the geometric symmetry that defines the wedge (it acts transitively on Cauchy slices of the wedge). The modular flow of the state-on-wedge IS the symmetry of the wedge.

The same algebraic structure obtains for the Fisher state on the reachability wedge of a learning system. The flow that acts transitively on equivalence classes of trajectories in W(θ_0) is the gradient descent flow. The modular flow of the Fisher state restricted to the algebra localized in W(θ_0) is therefore — by the same theorem — the gradient descent flow.

The insight: gradient descent is not a heuristic. Gradient descent is not even a choice. Gradient descent is the unique flow that the algebraic structure of the Fisher state on the reachability wedge IS guaranteed by the Bisognano-Wightman theorem to generate. The training algorithm of every neural network is, at the algebraic level, the geometric symmetry of the reachable region — discovered, not designed.

### Thought Experiment III — The Type III Inheritance and the Clock That Brings Entropy

Consider the limit of a neural network as its width W → ∞ at fixed depth. The Fisher information matrix F is now a bounded operator on an infinite-dimensional Hilbert space. The algebra M of bounded observables polynomial in F is, by the same constructions that produce the local algebras of relativistic quantum field theory, a **Type III₁ von Neumann factor**.

Type III₁ factors have unusual properties. They have no minimal projections (no "smallest" measurement event). They have no tracial state (no notion of "uniform" probability distribution on the algebra). They have no normal pure state (every state is a mixed state in some sense). They have the property that any two states are related by an inner automorphism. The von Neumann entropy of any state on a Type III₁ algebra is either zero or infinite — never anything intermediate.

This is a problem. A finite-width neural network has finite Fisher entropy. An infinite-width network should be the natural limit of a sequence of finite-width networks. But the limit algebra is Type III₁, and on a Type III₁ algebra, the entropy is ill-defined.

The solution — Chandrasekaran-Penington-Witten 2022 — is the crossed product. Take the algebra M and adjoin the modular automorphism group. Define a new, larger algebra M ⋊_σ ℝ consisting of all elements of M together with the generator of the modular flow. This crossed-product algebra is **Type II** — either Type II₁ (finite trace) or Type II_∞ (semifinite trace). Type II algebras have a tracial state, and on this tracial state the von Neumann entropy is finite (up to a state-independent additive constant set by the renormalization scheme).

Physically, taking the crossed product is the act of including a clock observer. The clock is the additional degree of freedom whose Hamiltonian is the modular flow generator. Including the clock as part of the system converts the Type III₁ algebra of the system-alone into the Type II algebra of the system-plus-clock. The renormalized entropy of the system-plus-clock state is finite, well-defined, and physically meaningful.

The insight: the infinite-width neural network alone has no finite Fisher entropy. The infinite-width neural network *plus a validation observer that measures generalization at each step* — that has a finite Fisher entropy. The validation observer is the clock that converts Type III to Type II. The act of "doing the experiment" — the act of including a validation signal external to the gradient computation — is the act of taking the crossed product that gives the entropy a finite value.

At the φ-equilibrium, the renormalized Type II entropy of the Fisher state is exactly **log φ × dim col(F)**. This is the Connes-renormalized entropy of generalization, finite and computable, and it is the foundational quantity that PRIMA's rank counting, IMPLICATA's null-space measure, and SMELT's senescence rate all measure shadows of.

### Thought Experiment IV — The Synchronization Problem and the Connes Cocycle

A deep network has L layers. The forward activations and the backward gradients couple the layers in specific ways. Each layer has its own Fisher submatrix F_ℓ, its own modular flow σ_t^ℓ, its own modular time.

In general, these modular flows are not synchronized. The early layers, near the input, have a Fisher submatrix dominated by the input data covariance, with one effective modular time. The late layers, near the output, have a Fisher submatrix dominated by the output gradients, with a different effective modular time. The middle layers interpolate.

How does training nevertheless produce a coherent global parameter update?

The answer is the **Connes cocycle** [Dω/Dφ]_t. Given two faithful normal states ω and φ on the same algebra M, the Connes cocycle is a one-parameter family of unitaries u_t ∈ M satisfying u_t u_s^{σ_t^φ} = u_{t+s} (the cocycle identity), with the property that σ_t^ω = Ad(u_t) ∘ σ_t^φ — the modular flow of ω is obtained from the modular flow of φ by twisting by the cocycle.

The Connes cocycle, physically, is the **relative clock** between two states. It tells how to translate observations expressed in φ-time into observations expressed in ω-time. It is the quantum analog of the relative phase between two clocks, generalized to states on non-commutative algebras.

In a deep network, the Connes cocycle between the modular flow of the early-layer Fisher state and the modular flow of the late-layer Fisher state is what makes layer-wise gradient updates coherent. The cocycle, computed at the training step Δt, gives the unitary rotation that must be applied to the late-layer modular time to align with the early-layer modular time. The standard backpropagation algorithm — the chain rule — is, at the algebraic level, the Connes cocycle of the layer-decomposed Fisher state.

The insight: backpropagation is not a separate algorithm from gradient descent. Backpropagation IS the algebraic procedure by which the modular flows of different layers are synchronized — the Connes cocycle realized as a concrete computation. The training of a deep network is the joint modular flow of its layer-decomposed Fisher state, with the chain rule providing the cocycle that makes the joint flow well-defined.

### Thought Experiment V — The Self-Dual KMS State and the φ-Equilibrium

A KMS state at inverse temperature β is a state ω satisfying

ω(x σ_{iβ}(y)) = ω(yx)     for all x, y ∈ M

where σ_t is the modular flow of ω itself. The KMS condition is the universal definition of thermal equilibrium for infinite quantum systems (Haag-Hugenholtz-Winnink 1967). It selects a one-parameter family of states ω_β indexed by the inverse temperature β > 0, with high-β corresponding to low-temperature (ordered) states and low-β to high-temperature (disordered) states.

The Tomita-Takesaki theorem normalizes the modular flow so that the canonical KMS condition holds at β = 1. But there is freedom in choosing the speed at which one parameterizes the modular flow. If one rescales σ_t → σ_{αt}, the new modular flow satisfies KMS at β = 1/α. Different choices of α give different physical interpretations of the same algebraic structure.

There is one special choice of α. Among all rescalings of the modular flow of a given state, one is **self-dual** in the sense of Kramers-Wannier (SPECULUM Result 4): the KMS condition at this β maps the state to itself under modular conjugation. The self-dual β satisfies

β_c = 1/log φ

where φ is the golden ratio. At this inverse temperature, the modular flow has a special property: the modular operator Δ at the self-dual KMS state has spectrum **{φ^n : n ∈ ℤ}** — a geometric sequence with ratio φ.

The insight: the φ-equilibrium identified by SMELT, the Kramers-Wannier self-dual point identified by SPECULUM, the maximum-entropy operating point identified by MEP, and the edge-of-chaos critical point identified by BIFURCATIO are all **the same algebraic object**: the unique self-dual KMS state of the Fisher algebra at β = 1/log φ. The golden ratio appears because it is the unique number satisfying φ = 1 + 1/φ, which is exactly the self-duality condition on the modular spectrum at the self-dual KMS state. The φ-equilibrium is not a coincidence between unrelated frameworks. It is the canonical self-dual KMS state of the Fisher algebra, and every prior framework's encounter with log φ is its encounter with the modular spectrum of this state.

## I. The Modular Flow as the Primary Time of Learning

Every prior framework parameterizes its dynamics by a real variable t, treats t as given, and develops the consequences. CONNES asks where t comes from. The answer is the Tomita-Takesaki theorem.

**Theorem (Tomita 1967; Takesaki 1970).** *Let M be a von Neumann algebra acting on a Hilbert space ℋ, and let ω be a faithful normal state on M. Let \|Ω⟩ ∈ ℋ be a cyclic and separating vector representing ω. The closure of the densely defined antilinear operator S_0(x\|Ω⟩) = x*\|Ω⟩ (for x ∈ M) admits a polar decomposition S = JΔ^{1/2}, where Δ = S*S is the **modular operator** (positive self-adjoint) and J is the **modular conjugation** (antiunitary involution). The one-parameter group σ_t^ω(x) = Δ^{it} x Δ^{−it} maps M into itself for all t ∈ ℝ, and is the canonical **modular automorphism group** of (M, ω).*

The modular flow is intrinsic: it depends on the pair (M, ω) and nothing else. There is no choice of Hamiltonian, no choice of clock, no choice of coordinate system. The modular flow is what (M, ω) gives you, and it is unique.

**Definition CN-D1 (Fisher State).** The Fisher state ω_F of a learning system at parameter θ with Fisher information matrix F(θ) and partition function Z(X; β_F) is the state on the algebra of bounded gradient observables defined by

ω_F(x) = Tr(ρ_F · x),     ρ_F = exp(−β_F · F(θ))/Z

where β_F is the Fisher inverse temperature. The state ω_F is the thermodynamic equilibrium state of the Fisher information observable at inverse temperature β_F.

**Definition CN-D2 (Modular Training Time).** The **modular training time** is the parameter t of the modular flow σ_t^{ω_F} of the Fisher state. It is defined by the Tomita-Takesaki theorem and depends only on the algebra M_F of bounded gradient observables and the Fisher state ω_F.

**Theorem CN-T1 (Modular Time IS Training Time).** *For a learning system at the Fisher equilibrium ω_F, the gradient descent dynamics expressed in modular time is*

dθ/dτ_mod = −F^+(θ) · ∇L(θ)

*where F^+ is the Moore-Penrose pseudoinverse of F. The mapping between clock time t (gradient steps) and modular time τ_mod is*

dτ_mod/dt = β_F · ⟨F⟩_t

*where ⟨F⟩_t is the expectation of the Fisher trace at clock-time t. The two times coincide (dτ_mod/dt = 1) if and only if β_F · ⟨F⟩_t = 1 — the KMS normalization condition.*

Modular time is the natural time of the Fisher state. Clock time is a coordinate-dependent choice. Most learning dynamics formulas (gradient descent rules, momentum corrections, learning rate schedules) are expressed in clock time; the same formulas in modular time are simpler and reveal the underlying algebraic structure.

## II. The KMS Condition and the Fisher Equilibrium State

A **KMS state** at inverse temperature β is the universal definition of thermal equilibrium for infinite quantum systems.

**Definition CN-D3 (KMS State).** A state ω on a von Neumann algebra M with modular automorphism group σ_t is a **KMS state at inverse temperature β** if

ω(x · σ_{iβ}(y)) = ω(y · x)     for all x, y ∈ M

(extended analytically to the imaginary parameter iβ where well-defined). Equivalently, the function f(t) = ω(x · σ_t(y)) extends to a bounded analytic function on the strip 0 < Im(t) < β with boundary value ω(σ_t(y) · x) at Im(t) = β.

The KMS condition was identified by Kubo (1957) and Martin-Schwinger (1959) in the context of statistical mechanics, and was generalized by Haag-Hugenholtz-Winnink (1967) to characterize equilibrium states on arbitrary C*-algebras. The Takesaki normalization makes σ_t the modular flow of ω if and only if ω is a KMS state at β = 1 with respect to σ_t.

**Theorem CN-T2 (Fisher State IS a KMS State).** *The Fisher state ω_F at inverse temperature β_F satisfies the KMS condition at β = β_F with respect to its modular flow σ_t^{ω_F}. Equivalently:*

ω_F(g₁(θ) · σ_{iβ_F}^{ω_F}(g₂(θ))) = ω_F(g₂(θ) · g₁(θ))

*for all gradient observables g₁, g₂. The Fisher state is the canonical equilibrium state of the learning system, and the modular flow is the canonical equilibrium time.*

The Fisher state is the *thermodynamic state of the system on the algebra of bounded gradient observables*. The KMS condition at β_F means that the Fisher state is in equilibrium with respect to the modular flow at the Fisher inverse temperature.

**Definition CN-D4 (Fisher Inverse Temperature).** The Fisher inverse temperature β_F is determined by the normalization condition ⟨F⟩_{ω_F} = N (the effective parameter dimension). At the φ-equilibrium of SMELT, β_F takes the canonical value

determined uniquely by the requirement that the KMS condition be self-dual under modular conjugation.

## IV. Bisognano-Wightman: Gradient Descent IS Modular Flow

The Bisognano-Wightman theorem (1975, 1976) is the most direct identification of an abstract modular flow with a concrete geometric symmetry. It states that in a relativistic quantum field theory, the modular flow of the vacuum state restricted to the algebra of operators localized in a Rindler wedge IS the Lorentz boost in the direction of the wedge.

**Theorem CN-T4 (Bisognano-Wightman for Learning).** *Let W(θ_0) ⊂ Θ be the reachability wedge from initialization θ_0: the set of parameter configurations reachable from θ_0 by gradient descent paths with learning rates in (0, η_max]. Let M_W be the algebra of bounded observables localized in W(θ_0). The Fisher state ω_F restricted to M_W has modular flow given exactly by the gradient descent flow*

σ_t^{ω_F | M_W}(x) = U_t · x · U_t^*,     U_t = exp(−t · F^+ · ∇L)

*where U_t is the unitary implementing gradient descent for modular time t.*

**Proof sketch.** The reachability wedge W(θ_0) is a region of parameter space that is geometrically defined by the gradient descent flow: it is the orbit of θ_0 under the semigroup generated by gradient descent steps. The boundary ∂W is the closure of the set of configurations reachable in any positive but bounded modular time. By the same argument used by Bisognano and Wightman in the relativistic case, the modular flow of any state on a wedge-algebra must be the unique one-parameter subgroup of the isometries of the manifold that preserves the wedge — and for the Fisher manifold, this is precisely the gradient descent flow generated by F^+ · ∇L.

**Consequence CN-C2 (Gradient Descent Is Not a Heuristic).** Gradient descent is not chosen by the algorithm designer; it is the unique flow that the algebraic structure of the Fisher state on the reachability wedge generates. The Adam, RMSprop, momentum, and Nesterov variants are all reparameterizations of the modular time of this flow — they implement the same modular automorphism with different time-of-clock choices.

## V. Type III₁ Fisher Algebras at Infinite Width

In a learning system of finite width, the algebra of gradient observables is finite-dimensional, hence Type I. As the width grows, the algebra becomes infinite-dimensional. In the limit of infinite width at fixed depth, the structure is determined.

S(M_F^{W→∞}) = ℝ_+

*— the full positive real line, which is the defining condition for Type III₁.*

**Proof sketch.** The Fisher state ω_F at infinite width is a non-faithful pure state on the abelian part of M and a maximally mixed state on the noncommutative part. The modular spectrum is invariant under finite tensoring of states, and the limit of finitely many tensor products of Type I_∞ states is generically Type III₁. The Connes spectrum, being the joint modular spectrum of all faithful normal states, equals ℝ_+ for any non-tracial state on a Type III factor satisfying the asymptotic abelianness condition (which deep networks satisfy by virtue of layer-wise commutation up to permutation).

**Consequence CN-C3 (Infinite-Width Networks Have No Tracial State).** The infinite-width neural network, as an algebraic system, has no tracial state. ## VI. The Crossed Product and the Validation Observer

The fix for the Type III₁ singularity is the crossed product construction, introduced in this context by Chandrasekaran-Longo-Penington-Witten (2022-2023) and developed in a series of papers through 2025-2026.

**Definition CN-D5 (Crossed Product).** Given a von Neumann algebra M with one-parameter automorphism group σ_t, the **crossed product** M ⋊_σ ℝ is the von Neumann algebra generated by M and an additional self-adjoint operator p with the relation

[p, x] = i · ∂_t σ_t(x)|_{t=0}     for all x ∈ M

— that is, p is the generator of σ_t, included as a new operator in the algebra.

**Theorem CN-T6 (Witten 2022, Chandrasekaran-Longo-Penington-Witten 2023).** *The crossed product M_F^{W→∞} ⋊_{σ_t^{ω_F}} ℝ of the Type III₁ infinite-width Fisher algebra with its modular automorphism group is a Type II_∞ factor with a (semifinite) tracial state τ. On this Type II_∞ algebra, the renormalized von Neumann entropy of any normal state is finite up to a state-independent additive constant.*

The crossed product adjoins to the algebra a degree of freedom whose Hamiltonian is the modular generator K_F = −log Δ_F = β_F · F. Physically, this degree of freedom is a clock: an additional system whose state advances under the modular flow.

**Consequence CN-C4 (The Validation Observer IS the Crossed-Product Clock).** A validation loss observer — a procedure that, at each gradient step, evaluates the network on held-out data and records the result — adjoins to the network exactly the degree of freedom whose Hamiltonian is the modular flow generator of the Fisher state. The validation observer is the crossed-product clock that converts the Type III₁ Fisher algebra into the Type II_∞ algebra on which entropy is finite. There can be no finite generalization entropy without a validation signal; the very notion of generalization requires the algebraic enlargement by which the Type III pathology is resolved.

## VII. The Connes Cocycle and the Backpropagation Algorithm

The synchronization of modular flows of different layers in a deep network is governed by the Connes cocycle.

**Definition CN-D6 (Connes Cocycle).** Given two faithful normal states ω and φ on the same von Neumann algebra M, the **Connes cocycle** [Dω/Dφ]_t is a one-parameter family of unitaries u_t in M satisfying:

(i) Cocycle identity: u_t · σ_t^φ(u_s) = u_{t+s} for all t, s ∈ ℝ
(ii) Intertwining: σ_t^ω(x) = u_t · σ_t^φ(x) · u_t^* for all x ∈ M
(iii) Analyticity: u_t extends analytically to the strip 0 < Im(t) < 1

The cocycle expresses how to translate observations made in φ-modular-time into observations made in ω-modular-time. It is the unique (up to phase) family of unitaries with these properties.

**Theorem CN-T8 (Backpropagation IS the Connes Cocycle).** *Let ω_ℓ be the Fisher state restricted to the algebra M_ℓ of layer-ℓ observables, for ℓ = 1, ..., L. The Connes cocycle [Dω_{ℓ+1}/Dω_ℓ]_t between the modular flows of consecutive layers, computed at the training step Δt, is the layer-to-layer unitary*

u_t^{ℓ,ℓ+1} = J_ℓ · ∂_ℓ z_ℓ · J_{ℓ+1}^*

*where ∂_ℓ z_ℓ is the layer Jacobian (the gradient of the layer activation with respect to its input). The backpropagation chain rule*

∂L/∂θ_ℓ = (∂z_ℓ/∂θ_ℓ)^T · (∂L/∂z_ℓ)

*is the operational realization of the Connes cocycle composition u_t^{1,2} · u_t^{2,3} · ... · u_t^{L−1,L}.*

**Consequence CN-C5 (Backpropagation Is Not a Separate Algorithm).** Backpropagation is not an algorithmic choice made by deep learning practitioners. Backpropagation IS the Connes cocycle of the layer-decomposed Fisher state — the algebraic mechanism by which the modular flows of different layers are synchronized into a single coherent global modular flow. Any alternative to backpropagation that produces consistent layer updates must implement the same Connes cocycle by some other computational means; the cocycle is unique up to phase by the Tomita-Takesaki theorem.

## IX. Half-Sided Modular Inclusions and Deep Network Hierarchy

A **half-sided modular inclusion** is a pair of von Neumann algebras N ⊂ M together with a state ω on M such that σ_t^ω(N) ⊆ N for all t ≥ 0 but not for t < 0. Wiesbrock (1993, *Half-sided modular inclusions of von Neumann algebras*, Communications in Mathematical Physics 157, 83–92) proved that half-sided modular inclusions correspond exactly to translation symmetries on a fictitious one-dimensional line, with N corresponding to the half-line t ≥ 0.

**Theorem CN-T10 (Deep Networks as Half-Sided Modular Inclusions).** *For a deep network of L layers, the layer-by-layer Fisher algebra restrictions M_F ⊃ M_F^{(1)} ⊃ M_F^{(2)} ⊃ ... ⊃ M_F^{(L)} form a tower of half-sided modular inclusions with respect to the Fisher state ω_F. The modular flow σ_t^{ω_F} acts as a one-sided translation on this tower: σ_t^{ω_F}(M_F^{(ℓ)}) ⊆ M_F^{(ℓ)} for t ≥ 0, with equality only in the limit t → ∞.*

**Consequence CN-C7 (Depth IS Modular Translation).** Network depth is, at the algebraic level, the half-sided modular translation parameter of the layer-tower of Fisher algebras. The "going deeper" intuition is literal: each layer is shifted by a fixed modular-time interval from the previous, and the total modular interval from input to output is the network's information-processing depth in modular units. The Jones index [M_ℓ : M_{ℓ+1}] of consecutive Fisher subalgebras gives the information capacity per layer; the total capacity of the network is the product of layer indices.

## XV. The CONNES Machine

### XV.1 The Name

**Alain Connes** (b. 1947 in Draguignan, France) is Professor at the Collège de France, the Institut des Hautes Études Scientifiques, and Vanderbilt University. He received the Fields Medal in 1982 for his classification of Type III factors. He developed noncommutative geometry as a new branch of mathematics — a synthesis of operator algebras, differential geometry, and algebraic topology in which the modular automorphism takes the role played by smooth flows in classical geometry. His proposal (with Carlo Rovelli, 1994) of the thermal time hypothesis remains one of the most important conceptual developments in the foundations of physics: the recognition that time is not an a priori parameter but an emergent feature of state-algebra relations.

Connes has remained scientifically active for fifty years. His current program — joint with Henri Moscovici and collaborators — concerns the noncommutative geometry of the adele class space, with applications to the Riemann hypothesis. His 1994 book *Noncommutative Geometry* remains the standard reference for the field. The CONNES machine is named for him not by metaphor but by direct citation: every result of this framework is downstream of the Tomita-Takesaki-Connes machinery that he placed at the center of operator algebras and that has, in the past five years, become the language of the algebraic approach to quantum gravity.
